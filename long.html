City terminates police chief

By Wade Young

03 · 31 · 2017

2:06 p.m. This story was updated to note that Pete Bulger is Suervisor in Charge in the Department

With a 4–1 decision after a special meeting Thursday, March 30, the Montgomery City Council terminated the contract with Police Chief John Schmidt.

The meeting opened at 7 p.m. Several administrative items were approved for a Czech May Day Celebration before it was closed at 7:35 p.m. to discuss an investigation and employee performance for Schmidt.

The council reopened the meeting at 8:20 p.m., and according to Open Meeting Laws, voted publicly to terminate Schmidt’s contract. Mayor John Grimm made the motion, which councilor Mick McGuire seconded.

When it came to a roll call vote, Grimm, McGuire, Dennis Lambrecht and Brad “Butch” Kubes voted in favor of terminating Schmidt. Councilor Bob Forcelle voted against it. The council did not reveal the subject/results of the investigation.

Schmidt was not present.

Officer Schmidt

Before he closed the meeting, Grimm saw that approximately 40 people were in

attendance, perhaps in response to a Facebook post that has since been deleted. The post by “MontgomeryMN Concerncitizens,” told people of an attempt to remove Schmidt from his position, then suggested people attend the meeting to support Schmidt. Grimm deviated from the agenda and permitted people to briefly speak up at the meeting.

When they voted, Grimm called it a “very difficult situation” and acknowledged that Schmidt is a “good guy.”

After the meeting adjourned, McGuire said the termination was effective immediately.

Schmidt has been the police chief since May 6, 2000. Prior to that, he had worked full time for the Montgomery force since 1993. He began with the city in 1991 as a part-time officer.

According to City Administrator Brian Heck, there had been other investigations on Schmidt from outside of the city.

“There’s not a whole lot I can disclose,” Heck said.

When asked if there had been any other disciplinary actions on Schmidt, Heck acknowledged, “There had been, yes.”





The administrator said he couldn’t provide information or dates on those investigations due to data privacy laws.

According to the Minnesota Statutes 13.43 Subd. 2 under public data, “The final disposition of any disciplinary action together with the specific reasons for the action and data documenting the

basis of the action...” is public. However, Heck declined to release that information because Schmidt can appeal the council’s decision.

Schmidt was put on administrative leave and told of the special meeting when Heck served him his notice in the afternoon of Monday, March 27.

Heck said the council has provided 15 business days to Schmidt to file an appeal. According to Mark Anfinson, attorney for the Minnesota Newspaper Association, if he doesn’t appeal at the end of the timeline, then the council’s decision becomes final and all documents relating to it become public.

Heck said Montgomery Sergeant Peter Bulger was moved to supervisor in charge.

On March 20, the council also held a closed meeting to discuss the

investigations and performance of Schmidt. When they reopened that meeting, Heck had suggested the City reach out to the sheriff’s department to help the City with Chief Law Enforcement services. Heck and Grimm then met with Sheriff Tietz and Chief Deputy Brett Mason to see if the county could help on a contract basis, starting the day after Schmidt was placed on administrative leave. At their regular meeting on Tuesday, April 4, the county approved a three-month contract for the Le

Sueur County Sheriff’s Department with Montgomery for “full supervision of the Montgomery Police Department.”

The council is looking into holding a special meeting to approve that contract.

The Messenger reached out to Schmidt for comment. Phone messages and social media messages were not returned.

The city employs three full-time officers, Sergeant Pete Bulger, patrolmen Patrick Nelson and Dan Siebsen. Part-time officers include Michael Longbehn, Herbert Moon, Drew Van Guilder, Gordon Prochaska, Jack Strand, Shelly Milton, and Hunter Hubner.

Heck said he is not aware of any other investigations or performance issues with any of the Montgomery officers.■
